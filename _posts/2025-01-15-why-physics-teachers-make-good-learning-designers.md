When I left the classroom to join an educational technology company in 2012, I assumed most of what I knew about teaching would become irrelevant. I was wrong.

The core skills of a physics teacher — decomposing complex systems, designing experiments that surface misconceptions, asking questions that reveal thinking rather than just answers — turned out to be exactly what good learning design requires.

## The Experiment Mindset

A physics teacher doesn't explain gravity by describing it. They drop things. They roll balls down ramps. They ask students to predict, observe, and reconcile the gap.

This is the inquiry cycle, and it maps almost perfectly to good instructional design:

1. **Prior knowledge activation** — What do learners already believe?
2. **Cognitive conflict** — Surface the gap between belief and reality
3. **Exploration** — Let learners construct understanding through experience
4. **Explanation** — Formalize and name what they've discovered
5. **Application** — Transfer to new contexts

When I started designing learning programs, I realized I'd been running this loop for years in the classroom. The medium changed; the method didn't.

## Debugging Mental Models

In physics, wrong answers are rarely random. They're systematic. A student who says a heavier object falls faster has a coherent mental model — it's just wrong. Good teaching means diagnosing that model before replacing it.

Learning designers face the same challenge. When a learner fails, the question isn't "what didn't they understand?" but "what do they currently believe, and why does that belief seem right to them?"

This diagnostic instinct — looking for the coherent story behind the error — is something physics training builds without explicitly teaching it.

## What the Classroom Doesn't Prepare You For

To be fair, the transition isn't frictionless. Classrooms are immediate. You get feedback in real time — a confused face, a question, a moment when the room shifts.

Learning products operate at a distance. You design for a learner you'll never meet, in a context you can't control. The feedback loop stretches from minutes to months.

That's a genuine adjustment. But the underlying craft — building experiences that change how someone thinks — is the same work.

---

If you're a teacher thinking about moving into learning design, trust what you already know. The classroom taught you more than you realize.
